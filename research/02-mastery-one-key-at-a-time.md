# 02 — One key at a time, to mastery (overlearning)

**Date:** 2026-07-05
**Question:** Introduce a whole row of keys at once, or one key at a time? And how
much practice does a key need before moving on?

## Findings
- **Automaticity comes from overlearning, not constant novelty.** Overlearning
  transitions processing from effortful/controlled to automatic retrieval.
- Touch-typing pedagogy is explicit: *"introduce a handful of keys at a time, and
  move on only once you've mastered them."*
- Standard classroom curricula follow a tight loop: ~two new keys per lesson →
  word drill → sentence drill → skills test → next.
- keybr's core innovation is introducing letters **one at a time**, gated on a
  per-letter confidence score.

## What this means for us
Adopted as the app's core mechanic (Option B): a single un-mastered **target** key
is drilled (interleaved with mastered keys, per topic 01) until it clears a per-key
gate, then graduates and the next key is introduced. Replaces the old "unlock a
whole row on accuracy alone" behavior. → Decision 02 (Adopted).

## Sources
- "Overlearning" overview (Grokipedia) — **ANECDOTAL / tertiary** (concept summary;
  underlying claims trace to standard cognitive psychology)
- how-to-type.com / readandspell.com touch-typing guidance — **COMMERCIAL** (mainstream pedagogy)
- Typing.com classroom Scope & Sequence — **COMMERCIAL** (curriculum standard)

## Confidence
**Strong** on the principle (overlearning → automaticity; master-before-advance is
near-universal pedagogy). The *specific* one-at-a-time cadence is keybr's design
choice, well-regarded but not proven superior to two-at-a-time by a controlled
trial we've found.
